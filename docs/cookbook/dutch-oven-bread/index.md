# Dutch Oven Bread

## At a Glance

- Yield: 1 dutch oven round loaf or two bread tins
- Rise time: at least 4 hours, ideally 12 hours
- Bake time: about 35 minutes

## Ingredients

- 1000 grams of flour
- 670 grams of water
- 1-2 tablespoons of yeast
- 1-2 tablespoons of salt

## Directions

1. Mix the ingredients together in the rising container. Use a wooden spoon to mix for a minute or so.
2. Cover with a cloth or a loose-fitting lid that lets air in and out without drying the bread out.
3. Let the dough rise for at least four hours. The longer the better. 12 hours is ideal, and over 24 hours might be a bit too long.
4. After rising, make a ball with clean hands and place it in the Dutch oven.
5. Let it rise for at least 30 minutes in about 80 F weather. Rise it longer for colder weather and shorter for hotter weather.
6. Consult a Dutch oven chart for how many coals to put on the bottom and top for the size of Dutch oven you have.
7. Cook the bread at around 375 F. Start hot with the number of coals you would use for 450 F or 475 F. Do this for eight minutes, then pull coals off and target 375 F.
8. After 35 minutes, use a thermometer to check the bread. Do not check early. Keep the lid on. You want the center of the bread to be 190 F.
9. Take the bread out of the Dutch oven and let it cool.

## Notes

- Ideally you want the center to be 190 F, but that target can be hard to hit. If the bread is 190 F around the middle edge and 175 F in the center, pull it off the coals and give it an extra minute or two. Do not wait too long because the temperature changes quickly.
- If the thermometer probe comes out with wet dough on it, it needs to cook more.
- Let the bread cool for 10 to 30 minutes before cutting it. If you cut it too early, it will be too wet in the center of each slice, and cutting it will smash the bread together. When it is drier, it will spring back into shape when you cut it.
- If you have problems with the bottom burning, the coals may not be placed correctly, may be too hot, or may be too many of them. One hack is to put some rice in the bottom of the Dutch oven. I have not tried this, but you can try it.
- Always use the same amount of flour, but change the amount of water for different bread outcomes. Anywhere between 650 and 700 grams of water works. The more water you use, the harder the dough is to work with, because it is stickier.
- Don't worry if the flour fully incorporates. It will as the yeast rises.
