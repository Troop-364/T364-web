# Dutch Oven Bread

## Recipe

- 1000 grams of flour
- 670 grams of water
- 1-2 tablespoons of yeast
- 1-2 tablespoons of salt

Mix the ingredients together in the rising container. Use a wooden spoon to mix. Mix for a minute or so. Cover with a cloth, or a loose fitting lid that will let air in and out, but not too much air. Don't dry the bread out. Let the bread rise for at least four hours, but the longer the better, 12 hours is ideal, over 24 hours might be a bit too long.

After rising, make a ball (using your clean hands), and place the ball in the dutch oven and let rise for at least 30 minutes in 80 degree weather. Longer for colder weather, shorter for hotter.

Consult a dutch oven chart for how many coals to put on the bottom and top, for the size of dutch oven you have.

We want to cook the bread at around 375 degrees. But we want to start hot. Start with the number of coals for 450 or 475.  Do this for eight minutes, then pull coals off and target 375. 

After 35 minutes, use the thermomotor to check the bread. Do not check early! Ideally, you want the center to be 190 degrees. Take the bread out of the dutch oven and let it cool.

Reality Check... ideally you want the center to be 190, but that target can be hard to hit. If the bread is 190 degrees around the middle edge and 175 in the center, pull it off the coals and give it an extra minute or two. But not too long, the temperature changes quickly. 

In any case, if the thermomotor probe comes out with wet dough on it, it needs to cook more.

Let the bread cool for 10 to 30 minutes before cutting it. If you try to early, it will be too wet in the center of each slice, and cutting it will smash the bread together. When it is dryer, it will spring back into shape when you cut it.

If you have problems with the bottoms burning, first your coals are not placed correctly, or are too hot, or have too many of them. But one hack is to put some rice in the bottom of the dutch oven. I have not tried this, but you can try it.


Note: Always use the same  amount of flour, but change the amount of water for different bread outcomes: anywhere between 650 to 700 grams of water. The more water the harder the dough is to work with.

Note: Don't worry if the flour fully incorporates, it will as the yeast rises.
